TRANSFER OF HEAT


Heat flows from a body at a higher temperature to a body at a lower temperature. This is called transfer of heat. Heat is transferred in three different ways:


1.Conduction
2.Convection
3.Radiation

1. Conduction:
Conduction is the transfer of heat from a hotter part to a colder part of an object due to the collision of its particles without actual movement of the particles from their position. How conduction works: When one end of a metal object is heated, the particles at that end gain energy and vibrate more. These vibrations are passed to adjacent particles, transferring heat along the object. For example, when a metal pan is heated, heat travels from the flame to the entire pan, making it hot.
Conductor :
Materials which allow heat to pass through them.
Insulator :
Materials which do not allow heat to pass through them.

2. Convection:
Convection is the process of heat transfer in liquids and gases, where heated particles move and carry heat with them. This movement creates currents, such as breezes near the sea or the rising of smoke, making convection a key process in nature. How convection works: When a liquid or gas is heated, its particles gain energy, become less dense, and rise. Cooler, denser particles move to take their place, creating a cycle of movement called a convection current.
Example:
When water is heated in a pan, the warmer water rises and cooler water sinks, distributing heat. Convection in gases: In air, heated air expands, becomes lighter, and rises. When these particles are heated, they become lighter than the surrounding air, causing them to rise. This is similar to what happens when air is heated—it expands, takes up more space, and becomes less dense, which is why warm air rises. Convection in liquids: In liquids like water, heated particles rise and cooler ones sink, creating a circular flow. Land Breeze and Sea Breeze During the day, the land near the beach heats up faster than the water in the sea. This is because different materials absorb heat at different rates. However, at night, the situation changes: the land cools down faster than the water. This difference in how quickly land and water heat up and cool down is what causes land and sea breezes.

Sea Breeze Formation: Land heats faster than water during the day, warming the air above it, which rises. Movement: Cooler, denser air from the sea moves in to replace the rising warm air, creating an onshore breeze. Characteristics: Cool, moist, and stronger than land breezes, bringing relief to hot coastal areas. Land Breeze Formation: Land cools down faster than the sea at night, making the air above it cooler and denser. Movement: Air flows from the high-pressure land towards the warmer, lower-pressure sea, creating an offshore breeze. Characteristics: Drier and generally weaker than sea breezes.

3. Radiation:
Radiation is heat transfer that does not require any medium. All objects emit heat in this way. Heat transfer happens directly from a hot object to us through a process called radiation.
